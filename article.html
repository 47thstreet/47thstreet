Cover Story

The Last
Master Cutter

Inside the Workshop Where Rough Becomes Radiance

By Sarah Chen April 15, 2026 12 min read
An idealized round brilliant cut, the geometry Friedman has perfected over five decades. Illustration by 47th Street.

The elevator in 15 West 47th Street is the kind that announces itself before it arrives — a low mechanical groan, then the reluctant parting of brass doors that have been opening and closing since the Eisenhower administration. Moshe Friedman rides it every morning at seven forty-five, a full hour before most of the building's other tenants even think about unlocking their booths. He carries the same battered leather case he has carried for fifty-three years, its contents worth more than most apartments in the borough: a set of hand-ground steel dops, two loupes of different magnification, and whatever rough stone he's been studying the night before, turning it under his desk lamp the way another man might turn the pages of a novel.

"You have to understand," he says, settling into the chair behind his wheel, "the stone already knows what it wants to become. My job is to listen."

It is a sentiment that sounds almost mystical, and Friedman is aware of how it plays. He is not a mystical man. He is, by his own accounting, a technician — one who happens to possess a spatial intuition so refined that other cutters on the block speak of it the way musicians speak of perfect pitch. Where a younger cutter might rely on a Sarin machine to map a rough diamond's internal geometry, Friedman can rotate a stone between his thumb and forefinger and tell you, within fractions of a degree, the optimal angle for the table facet. He learned this from his father, who learned it from his father, who brought the craft out of Antwerp in 1941 with nothing but his tools and a letter of introduction to a dealer on the Bowery.

Three generations of accumulated knowledge, refined by tens of thousands of hours at the wheel. And when Friedman retires — which he says will be soon, though he has been saying this for a decade — there is no one positioned to carry it forward.

“The stone already knows what it wants to become. My job is to listen.”

The crisis is not unique to Friedman. Across the Diamond District, the average age of a master cutter has crept past sixty-five. The apprenticeship model that sustained the trade for centuries — young men entering workshops at sixteen, spending years on calibration stones before being trusted with anything of value — has effectively collapsed. The reasons are manifold and mutually reinforcing. The economics of cutting have shifted overseas, to Surat and Guangzhou, where labor costs make Manhattan workshops uncompetitive for bread-and-butter goods. The cultural pipeline that once fed young Orthodox men into the trade has diversified, with the next generation increasingly drawn to technology, finance, or the professions. And the tools themselves have changed: computer-guided laser cutting can now execute with mechanical precision what once required a decade of tactile learning.

But speak to the dealers who have watched Friedman work, and they will tell you that what the machines produce and what Friedman produces are categorically different things. "A laser can cut a diamond," says Ravi Chandiramani, who has been brokering stones on the block for thirty years. "Moshe can make a diamond sing. There is a quality — call it life, call it fire, call it whatever you want — that comes from a human hand understanding light at an intuitive level. The machines are getting closer. They are not there yet."

Friedman's workshop is a study in deliberate anachronism. The cutting wheel is a modern Coborn, yes, but the dops are hand-ground to specifications his father established in the 1950s. The lighting rig is his own design, a north-facing arrangement of full-spectrum LEDs that he spent two years calibrating to replicate the quality of natural daylight at this latitude. On the wall behind his bench, a yellowed photograph shows his grandfather at a nearly identical setup in Antwerp, circa 1938 — the same posture, the same tilt of the head, the same fierce concentration narrowing the eyes to slits.

"People ask why I don't teach," Friedman says. He pauses, turns the stone he's been examining — a 4.2-carat piece of rough from Botswana that a dealer has asked him to assess for cutting potential. "The truth is, I tried. Three times I took on apprentices. The first lasted eight months. The second, almost two years. The third —" he pauses again, and something crosses his face that might be regret. "The third was talented. Really talented. But he could make more money in a week trading crypto than I could pay him in a month. I don't blame him. The economics don't work."

The economics, in fact, tell a stark story. In 1985, there were an estimated two hundred master cutters working on 47th Street. Today, the Diamond District Workforce Survey counts fewer than thirty, and more than half are past retirement age. The stones that once would have been cut in Manhattan are now shipped to India, where Surat's cutting district employs some 800,000 workers and processes roughly ninety percent of the world's diamonds by volume. What remains on 47th are the exceptional pieces — large stones, unusual rough, gems that demand the kind of bespoke attention that a factory floor cannot provide. These are the stones that still find their way to Friedman's bench, and they are the stones that have earned him a reputation that extends far beyond the block.

Among his known works: the recutting of a 12-carat oval from the Golconda mines that had been poorly proportioned in the nineteenth century, restoring it to a fire that its original cutter had only gestured toward. A 6-carat emerald-cut stone that appeared on the hand of a sitting Supreme Court justice and was later described by the Gemological Institute of America as "among the finest examples of emerald-cut proportioning in the modern era." And, most famously, a 22-carat rough stone from the Jwaneng mine that Friedman studied for six weeks before making his first cut, ultimately producing a 9.8-carat round brilliant that sold at Christie's for $4.2 million and set a per-carat record for its color grade.

On a Tuesday afternoon in late March, Friedman is doing what he does most afternoons: studying rough. The Botswana stone sits in a small porcelain dish under his north-light rig, and he rotates it with a pair of brass tweezers, pausing every few degrees to peer through his loupe. He is looking for inclusions — the tiny imperfections that determine how a rough stone can be divided and shaped. A modern cutter would scan the stone with a Sarin DiaScan, generating a three-dimensional map of its internal structure in seconds. Friedman has a DiaScan in his workshop. He uses it to confirm what his eyes have already told him.

"The machine sees the inclusion," he explains, holding the stone up to the light. "I see the inclusion and I see how the light bends around it. I see where the fire will be, and where it won't. That is the difference between cutting a diamond and cutting a great diamond."

Whether that difference will survive Friedman's retirement is the question that hangs over 47th Street like the perpetual haze of cigarette smoke that once defined it. The Diamond District has always been a place that reinvents itself — from the refugee workshops of the 1940s to the Hasidic trading floors of the 1970s to the Instagram showrooms of today. Each transformation has carried losses alongside its gains. The master cutter may simply be the latest casualty of an industry that has never stood still.

But Friedman, characteristically, is not sentimental about it. "The diamonds will still be beautiful," he says, returning the stone to its dish. "Maybe not in the same way. But beautiful." He reaches for his loupe again, holds it to his eye, and bends back over the light. The elevator groans in the hallway. Another day on 47th Street is winding down. But Moshe Friedman is not finished yet.
